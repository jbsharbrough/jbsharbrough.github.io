CLOSER LOOK: Jay Powell

I admit that I like the way that Cowboy Jay Powell pitches when he's hot, and I don't like the way he pitches when he's not. This season, which has included time off for the flu, has been an inconsistent one. When we look at Jay Powell and Colby Lewis, we see a similar record, but Nat Showalter is handling Powell differently from the way that he handled Esteban Yan and Todd Van Poppel. Why?

Showalter was quoted a number of times in the spring about the amount of faith he places in a "track record." Lewis doesn't have one. Powell does.

When Yan and Van Poppel were released, the papers made mention of the number of appearances in which they allowed runs.  Based on that, I've been waiting for the sword to fall on Powell, too. He's allowed runs in bunches. When he gave up three runs on 12 Jun, I was sure that he would be gone the next day. To that point, in 17 appearances, he had allowed runs in 11 of them. Twice this year, he had stuggled so badly that he had allowed 5 runs in a single appearance. His ERA of 11.80 was bound to break someone's spreadsheet. He was allowiing an opposing on base average of almost .500, and a slugging percentage over .600.

I was wrong. Showalter not only kept Powell, he sent him out the next two nights. Powell responded by retiring the only hitter he faced on Friday, and the three hitters that he faced on Saturday. Granted, neither game was close, but it was good to see him get out there and get people out.

Powell has not allowed a slugging percentage over .417 in the past five years. His career opposing OBA is .351. It seems that, for the moment, the Rangers have more faith in him than they had in Yan, Andy Benes, and Van Poppel. It's possible that the management believes that if he pitches well for the next month, he might have some trade value. Hicks has indicated that he might pick up some salary if it results in prospects. It appears that Jay is a good guy, who is not pitching consistently. Maybe next week, he'll be on the Hot List.
