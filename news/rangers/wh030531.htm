Here's an example of how hard it is to be consistent in the major leagues. From 1 May to 17 May, Carl Everett was on fire. From 18 May to the end of the month, he was not. This isn't a black and white change in his offensive production, but it's a hot and cold contrast. Carl is winning his way into manager Buck Showalter's heart, and the papers today indicate that they might include him in their plans past this season.

Note that in his hot half, he had some games where he didn't hit - okay, 1 game. In his cold half, he still hit in 6 of 10 games.  Overall, during May, his stats were remarkably steady - meaning that the season averages were not changed a great deal between the beginning and end of the season. His power dropped, but his OBA and BA at the end of the month were within 5 points of their beginning of the month levels.

Carl will be hot again later in the season. Baseball is a streaky game, where people (and teams) get hot and cold. The best players (and teams) average that out to a high level of performance. Carl's May offense is an example of a ballplayer who keeps something going even when he's not hot.

Next week, unless something changes, we'll look at Michael Young's emergence as a hitter this season. Here's a hint: in May, Carl had 1 game of 3 or more hits. Michael Young had 4. He was very hot until the final week of May.
