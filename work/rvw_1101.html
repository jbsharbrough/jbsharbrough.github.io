Lessons in Increasing Intelligence

Article from Robert Sternberg, PhD, IBM professor of psychology and education in the dept of psychology at Yale.

Intelligence tests don’t work because they measure a narrow range of abilities, and those abilities “are inert -- they don’t predict or lead to actions or commonsense mental skills necessary to achieve life’s important goals.  They also don’t measure your ability to think.”

Sternberg’s keys to “successful intelligence” include:
Analytical thinking -- the ability to solve problems and judge ideas.
Creative thinking -- the ability to formulate new or clever solutions to problems.
Practical thinking -- the ability to use your ideas and implement them effectively.
He recommends a balance among these three aspects.  Steps to developing them follow.

ANALYTICAL INTELLIGENCE

Recognize that there is a problem.
Define the problem.
Represent information about a problem accurately.
Invest resources.
Allocate resources wisely.
Track your progress during the problem-solving process.

CREATIVE INTELLIGENCE

Actively seek out and plan to become a role model.
Question assumptions and encourage others to do so, too.
Take sensible risks and encourage others to do the same.
Allow yourself and others to make mistakes.

PRACTICAL INTELLIGENCE

Recognize your pattern of strengths and weaknesses.
Strengthen those skills in which you excel.
Believe in yourself.

[How’s it relate to us and our work?  Measuring the ability to think could be very useful.  If you are designing a smart company and a learning company, being able to assess the thinking capacity of your staff is important.  Analyzing the different kinds of intelligence is one method of getting to a method of assessment that could be useful.]

Baby Boomers and the Coming Investment Boom

Baby Boomers are likely to continue the investment boom, bringing the Dow to 18,000 in about a decade.  There is a strong correlation between the S&P500 and the number of people in their peak spending years:  which peaks at age 46.  As people get that old, they begin to same more.  Between 2003 and 2009, the market should boom.  Then it may bust.

[This is a great example of linear thinking.  There are so many reasons why it might not come to pass.  Then again, I thought that pre-paid phone cards would be a stupid idea.]

The top performing funds of 1997...

are likely to be in last year’s least popular categories.  Funds in the categories that grew the least on a percentage basis in one year outperformed the average fund over the next one-, two- and three-year periods 79% of the time.  They outperfomed the most popular funds 92% of the time.

[This is more linear thinking.  A clearer understanding of the relationship between the cause and effect might come in really handy.]

No More Procrastination

Change procrastination into action by challenging yourself to get a task done in less than a half-hour ... and then committing yourself to an even shorter time period for the next task.

Or ... promise yourself that you will start working on a project for just 10 minutes and then quit if you get bored. [This one works for me]
If you continue to have trouble getting started, figure out why.  You may need clarification or help from someone else.
Reward yourself for completing parts of any project -- perhaps by taking a walk ... spending a few minutes reading an article that interests you ... or taking another type of short break.
