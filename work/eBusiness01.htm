eBusiness:  What is it anyway?

The new paradigm: eBusiness changes everything.
Kiss your geographic advantage goodbye.

From the chairmen of IBM, Intel, and GE to the daily news, managers are surrounded by those trumpeting the arrival of the eBusiness revolution. Every firm must join or perish. It’s been labeled everything from disruptive technology to new age of man. Is there anything to it? Do managers have time to react, or is it already too late? What concrete steps can be taken to benefit, and to avoid losses?

First, it would be good if we could understand a little more about what it is. In the strictest sense, eBusiness is a term that includes using technical infrastructure developed relatively recently to do business with customers, suppliers, and internally in ways that produce new competitive advantages. There are examples of firms that have negative working capital, but market capitalization in the tens of billions of dollars. There are examples of industry leading firms whose employees don’t take any action on the majority of their orders. It’s all handled by customers, vendors, and computers. A new verb has been created: managers wonder if they will be "Amazoned" by some new Internet company that will offer competing products and services at a greatly reduced price.

If you believe the papers, you should be buying hardware, software and services to manage your supply chain, procure items electronically, manage quality systems, do the entire enterprise resource planning cycle, sell on the web, automate customer service, market interactively, forecast demand, and manage orders. There isn’t really an integrated solution that addresses all of these applications available, but there are some very expensive pieces that you can knit together with some expensive consulting. Many large firms are trying to be first, and paying dearly for it.

eBusiness is:

Getting an internet strategy, not just realizing that you need one.
Looking outward, not only inward.
Developing new markets.
Entering a new league of customer service.
Selling information, at a margin well in excess of the one you get for products.
Focusing on service, not products.
Becoming more transparent to your suppliers and customers.
Communicating internally at a level not found before.
Intercompany trade. Far more than retail sales. Lots of intercompany trade.
Not just a website that looks like the sales brochure, or a shopping cart program.
Value chain management.
Intercompany collaboration for one-stop shopping on the net.
A greater emphasis on flexible pricing versus fixed pricing for goods and services.

The real revolution, as IBM Chairman Louis Gerstner described, is when the big battalions move. When Wal-Mart, IBM, GE, or Exxon decide to enter a new market, many other firms, including many early adopters of eBusiness strategies, will exit the marketplace. The clock is ticking, for many firms, and the outcome is already determined. Two areas that are holding the big guys back are language and security. It appears that XML will solve the language problem as soon as a given industry can come to agreement on definitions. Security is another matter entirely, and the end is not clearly in sight. Without totally secure financial transactions and information stores, buyers and sellers will stay on the beach.

A survey of Internet business in the Economist formulated the Internet Imperative: Use the disruptive technologies of digital networking or fall victim to those who do. This produced four corollaries.

  1. You need an e-business strategy.
  2. You have to change your culture to become more transparent.
  3. You should put the right technical infrastructure in place.
  4. You must start now.

Be warned: you will risk your reputation, your brand, and your customers when you put your business on the web.
