Discoveries, July 14, 1997

by Beau Sharbrough
The Discoveries section of the Dallas Morning News often features articles of relevance to non-linear dynamic systems.  The various sections are here identified, and where there is special interest, reviewed.


SPACE

MISSION ACCOMPLISHED: Pathfinder teaching scientists about Red Planet's wonders

SCIENCE UPDATE

Ants appear to let guard down to allow bees to pollinate trees. Certain flowers send out a signal to their guardian ants to bug off for a few hours, allowing bees to pollinate them.
Age when language is learned may affect how brain stores it. A study shows that a language is stored in a different part of your brain if it is learned at a different age. If you learn two languages at an early age, they are stored in the same place.
Child-care centers often don't position babies safely for sleep. About half of the centers risk SIDS episodes by letting infants sleep on their stomachs.
Study of clogged arteries in mice may yield new genetic clues about obesity. Mice gain weight without overeating in the absence of these new-found genes. The same mechanism will be sought in humans. [I find it difficult to believe that people or mice gain weight unless they eat more than they burn.]
Matters of the heart. Two recent studies suggest that genetics not only influences who has heart attacks, but how well people do after them.
Information inundation. Some Maryland biologists at the Institute for Genomic Research in Rockville just released 40 million letters of genetic code, containing information from 11 different organisms. It was the larges release of genetic information ever, and came one day after the institute ended its alliance with Human Genome Sciences. The change in relationship allowed the release of the information. [Those of you expecting the second album to be as good as the first one, just remember Christopher Cross.]

PHYSIOLOGY

Estrogen called key in men's bone development. Until recently, scientists thought that in men, testosterone was responsible for ending the adolescent growth spurt and for maintaining men's dense bones. Estrogen was thought to do the same in women. Surprise! A 38 year old man in Italy was still growing, and his bones were soft. His system produced no estrogen. During estrogen treatments, his bones hardened up like normal. [It is not yet widely known, but women produce small amounts of male sex hormone, and vice versa. The role of these hormones is still being discovered, as in this case.]

SIEGFRIED

Pathfinder mission is worth its weight in Martian money
This article includes the top ten reasons why sending Pathfinder to Mars was not a waste of money:

10. It cost less than making movies about going to Mars.
9. Viewers living in cities with cable companies wise enought to carry the NASA channel had somethings to watch on TV for a change.
8. Great pictures are now available for the next editions of Mars geology textbooks.
7. For a few days everybody forgot about OJ.
6. Mars is a safer place for robots than Mir is for astronauts.
5. It diverted attention from Roswell's alien landing anniversary.
4. It scoped out a good location for the sequel to Men in Black.
3. The weather there was better than in Dallas for a Fourth of July weekend.
2. Scientists are now happy for the first time since Jupiter got blasted by a comet.
1. It found a good place to send Mike Tyson.

A section justifying the cost of the mission: "In the end, when you add up the costs, Pathfinder's price tag came to $266 million, just about a dollar per American. As AP science editor Matt Crenson pointed out in his story, people 'spend eight times that much to see fake dinosaurs eat character actors.' (He might have added that some people will spend 50 times as much to see one boxer try to eat another one.)" [They don't call it pugilism for nothing.]

But seriously, folks, Mr Siegfried says that some of the other things that you get for your dollar are: "Knowledge about another world. Insight into the excitement generated when curiosity is harnessed in the pursuit of understanding nature. The satisfaction of knowintg that the spirit of exploration still infects the human race, keeping it alive -- and making the human brain worth having."
