eBusiness:  What is it anyway?

Welcome to the revolution: Points to remember under pressure.

Changed relationship between the value of core competencies and the value of geographic location

There is not much difference in the cost to the firm, or to the customer, of doing business across the world versus across town. That two edged sword works for the firm and all of its competitors. First, you can do business in geographic places where you could not provide services cost effectively or competitively before. Second, your distant competitors can do the same to you. Third, there is a greater emphasis on knowing a firm’s core competencies and using them for competitive advantage.
 

A significant distinction between the information content of a firms activities and the material content
A systematic investigation of a firm’s activities will show that some tasks are about moving information (shipping bits), others are about moving things (shipping atoms). The information can be moved almost instantly to almost everywhere. Some firms are using this approach to gain a competitive advantage.

That’s what allows firms to put order entry and customer service on the web, for example, and handle more people. Cisco has had a six fold increase in sales since 1994, but has only doubled the customer support staff. A manufacturing company like Dell spends a fraction of what a steel maker or auto maker spends to produce an additional dollar in sales. Leveraging information technology to lower the cost of doing business is not a new phenomenon, but it is a current opportunity for competitive advantage. This advantage will be less pronounced when every other firm in an industry adapts the approach.

The low incremental cost of supplying additional copies of packaged information allows managers to focus on opportunities for "increasing returns." A firm like Microsoft can produce an additional dollar of sales without tying up any additional capital.
 

The economic advantage of using mechanical agents to perform tasks previously performed by people

There are two kinds of advantage to using these robots: lower unit costs and faster information feedback cycles. It costs 1 cent to do a bank transaction on the internet, 27 cents with an ATM. Airline tickets can be sold for $1 on the web, $8 otherwise.

A minimum wage full time employee costs a firm about $250 per week in operating expenses. Most of them have computers on their desks that can do 80% of the work without them. Let’s face it – very little of the work that people do requires a person’s unique judgement, insight, and creativity. It involves drudgery, translating orders from voice to data, entering numbers that came from some other computer, processing transactions according to strict rules. Eventually, all humans will be doing one of four jobs: clown, judge, programmer, or sales clerk. Most likely you’ll get a random assignment each week telling you which one you have to do.

Automation has not produced the benefits that we expected in the 1960s. Still, the near term will see a significant reduction in the cost of simple transactions because people won’t be involved – robots will.
 

An opportunity to create a new exchange in a market that is fragmented or has no dominant player, and to create an infomediary where there the conditions for an exchange don’t exist.

The internet changes the buyer and seller relationship. Buyers can easily choose between a number of sellers, if the information is available. Sellers can’t be relied on to provide this information objectively. This creates a market opportunity for an "infomediary" to gather information and provide a platform for buyers and sellers to do business. eBay, Priceline, and a number of other firms have already identified markets for themselves. With so many trucks empty, hotel rooms empty, and plane seats empty, spot markets, with flexible prices, will proliferate for some time.
And in some cases, the infomediary will become an "Exchange." They’ll be the host of a new marketplace that didn’t exist before. There is a lot of money to be made when you can get a percentage of every transaction that takes place in a market.
 

The outfitters make more money than the miners.

Don’t forget, if you’re a miner, that the outfitters made more money in the gold rush than the miners did. Yes, there’s gold out there. Still, try to plan well enough that you don’t get gouged by the outfitters.
