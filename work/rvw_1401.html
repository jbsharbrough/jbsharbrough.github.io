CHANGE

Levi’s

At Levi-Strauss, they did something as revolutionary as I can imagine.  A $7 billion dollar company redesigned the entire organization - all new jobs, functions, the whole nine yards.  Then every employee interviewed for one of the new jobs.  Some employees tried for their first and second choices and failed.  This was very disturbing to them, and was the biggest negative associated with the program.  The positives were that they learned a lot about the people that they had - I didn’t know she had a degree in chemistry! - and that each employee was forced to ask themselves where and how they could create value in the organization.

The book of the new company was not generally circulated.  Employees who requested it got it.

On dealing with resistance to change, there are three rules:

Don’t expect effective online communication without extensive face-to-face communication.
There’s no such thing as giving people too much information.
Business is personal.  People commit themselves to other people, not to organizations.
Small gestures send big signals -- especially when they relate to core principles.
The essence of leadership is authenticity.
Talk softly about what you’re doing and carry a big ruler to measure real results.
Strong leaders want strong sidemen.
It’s a pretty basic concept:  if you’re prepared to do whatever it takes to satisfy your customers and you’re willing to extend the benefits of success to your people, you can achieve extraordinary results.
A brand is a promise, and you have to keep your promises.  There’s no difference between what we sell and who we are.
If you do not aggressively, tangibly, and visibly manage time in your organization, you’re history.
Expect it.
Don’t take it personally.
Understand that resistance comes in code.
When a disappointed employee cried, she got two messages.  First, don’t let this take away from what you’ve accomplished.  You’ve contributed a lot to this company.  Second, think more broadly about your role here.  Many of your skills are transferable.  Why are you limiting yourself?  Where else can you add value?

The most important advice:  “Find where you can add value in your company and go after it.  It may not be in the job you’re in now.  It may not be in the job you like best.  But that’s what matters -- adding value.  That’s where you find security and fulfillment.”

What Comes After What Comes Next

How can you develop your ability to anticipate the future?
LEARN TO BE A BETTER LISTENER.  You don’t listen with your mouth open.  If you sell Kool Aid, don’t hang out at the grocery store when the kids are in school.
ONCE A WEEK, READ A TRADE MAGAZINE FROM A DIFFERENT INDUSTRY.  Find two things that relate to your business or provoke your thinking.
LET YOUR KIDS TUTOR YOU IN A SUBJECT THEY KNOW MORE ABOUT THAN YOU DO.  It’s humbling.  You don’t have all the answers, and you should listen to people that you never thought could teach you anything.
VOLUNTEER.  This is the great equalizer - all volunteers are peers.  It strips away the power, and changes your perspective on hierarchy and authority.
READ WHAT HAS STOOD THE TEST OF TIME.  Aristotle, Shakespeare, Adam Smith, The Federalist Papers.  People still put fresh flowers on the grave of Leopold  the First.  Isn’t that the impact you want to have on your company?  Reading things that last 300 years helps frame thinking that lasts 300 years.

The Trillion-dollar Vision of Dee Hock

Associates:  Hire and promote first on the basis of integrity; second, motivation; third, capacity; fourth, understanding; fifth, knowledge; and least and least, experience.
Compensation:  Money motivates neither the best people, nor the best in people.  It can move the body and influence the mind, but it cannot touch the heart or move the spirit; tha is reserver for belief, principle, and morality.
Form and Substance:  Preserve substance, modify form.  The closest thing to a law of nature in business is that form has an affinity for expense, while substance has an affinity for income.
Leadership:  40% yourself, 30% your boss, 15% your peers.  Use the remained to teach the same theory to your subordinates.  Lead yoursefl, lead your superiors, lead your peers, and free your people to do the same.
Organization:  An organization’s success has enormously more to do  with clarity of a shared purpose, common principles and strength of belief in them than to assets, expertise, operating ability, or management competence.
The organization must be adaptable and responsive to changing conditions, while preserving overall cohesion and unity of purpose.
The trick is to find the delicate balance that allows the system to avoid turf fights and back-stabbing on the one hand, and authoritarian micromanagement on the other.
The organization must cultivate equity, autonomy, and individual oportunity.
The organization’s governing structure must distribute power and function to the lowers level possible.
The governing structure must not be a chain of command, but rather a framework for dialogue, deliberation, and coordination among equals.

LEARN

XBS Learns to Grow

“Think about it.  There are other companies in the outsourcing business who can offer equipment, supplies, and people.  Our only differentiator is the kind of community we are... It’s self-evident.  That’s why we’ve got to create an organization where everyone has a lot of knowledge of the business.”

What does it take to have an organization with that kind of knowledge?  “You start by changing how you think about the organization.  I think of our organization as a dynamic, living system, like an environment.  You can’t treat a natural system like a machine.  All you can do is create experience that disturb a natural system, and then it decides how to respond.  It’s more a amtter of offering people different perspectives and influencing their thinking than trying to drive them.”

It sounds indirect.  Why not a more top-down approach?  “When I got this job, my assignment was to create an empowerment strategy.  It turns out you can’t empower anyone.  This is not the freeing of the slaves.  The only thing that makes sense to me is to think of empowerment as an outcome of organizational conditions.  You create an environment for learning, with empowerment as an outcome.  When you start to think like that, you go from an empowerment strategy to a change strategy to where we are now -- it’s just our business strategy.”

What are the core principles of the change program?  “Let’s be clear:  it’s never been a ‘program.’  And unlike most other companies, we’ve never had a big change team.  We just had some things we wanted to try.  We knew the first piece of our change strategy -- to create a shared vision.  Bit I never thouhgt of it as a written vision statement.  To me, a vision is an ongoing conversation ... It’s never frozen, it’s never set.”

So how did you go about creating a shared vision?  “We did ‘visioning exercises” and learned how to have conversations together.  We got a group of 120, then a conference with 400.  Our vision became a living thing, not a plastic card.  Ask the people, ‘What’s a shared vision?’  They’ll tell you, ‘A shared vision is the way we talked with each other when we got together in Orlando.’”

What came after creating a shared vision?  “Trust.  Trust is one of the essentials for learning.  Stephen Covey’s idea is that people have emotional bank accounts.  You create trust through deposits.  I think when we started we were probably running a deficit.  We simply hadn’t done much for the people in the organization to build trust, to make deposits in an emotional account..  I can see the results of out conference on this today.  When the policy committee is considering a decision, you’ll hear someone ask, ‘Will the people in our organization see this as a deposit or a withdrawal?'  ‘Will it build trust or diminish trust?’”

Eight Principles for Learning:
1. Learning is fundamentally social.
2. Cracking the whip stifles learning.
3. Learning needs an environment that supports it.
4. Learning crosses heirarchical bounds.
5. Self-directed learning fuels the fire.
6. Learning by doing is more powerful than memorizing.
7. Failure to learn is often the fault of the system, not the people.
8. Sometimes the best learning is unlearning.
