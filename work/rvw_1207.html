Primate study finds grape hunt makes a monkey of computer.  A computer trying to find grapes, similar to the mouse in the maze GA, did not fare better than monkeys in this trial.  The monkeys were working on the “traveling salesman” problem - no jokes will be necessary.  Researchers hid grapes in holes in a grid while the monkeys watched them, and then let the monkeys develop their own search rules to get the grapes.

SIEGFRIED

Humans could learn new tricks from animal’s decision making
Dr Peter Shizgal of Concordia University, in Current Opinion in Neurobiology, says that animals make choices based on “hard wired” values, “as if they are anything but indifferent to the consequences of their actions.”  It would appear that their classifier rules, in his opinion, are all based on very short term values - that they ignore everything but current inputs.

The conclusion of the article:  “Someday, such knowledge may offer hope of improving human decision making.  In the meantime, animals will remain the best models making sound assessments leading to prompt decisions.  Except of course, for cats, who can never decide whether they want to be indoors or out.”  I think that the assessment of their decision strategy is way to generalized, and too simplistic.  It allows for no memory, and little learning by the individual.  I find that inconsistent with my own view of animal behaviour above the insect level, and a bad example for assessing human choices and methods of choosing.
