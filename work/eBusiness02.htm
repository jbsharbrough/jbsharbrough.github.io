The new senses: measurement and reporting for the future.
Making the blind see and the deaf hear.

John Holland’s pioneering work in complex adaptive systems (CAS) yields a simplified cycle of sensation, calculation, and action. In Hidden Order, we learn about any number of creatures who gather information from the environment through "sensors." They act on the environment through "effectors." The choice of the effector is based on rules, for which values are constantly re-assigned based on experience. These building blocks allow modeling of CAS behavior.

One of the most fascinating discoveries that Holland made was that more specific rules have greater value than less specific rules. An example might be the difference in rules that apply to all creatures moving toward you, and rules that apply to creatures moving toward you that you enjoyed eating in the past. A similar example would be the relative value of identifying red berries versus identifying the red berries that made you sick last month. The most modern one would be the ability of a female human to tell the difference between an attractive male human and an attractive male human with a Corvette.

A related point about the computation that CAS perform is that the information extracted from the environment should be fresh. It doesn’t help as much to realize that a large bird is swooping down on you if the realization is based on observations from two minutes ago. This concept relates to the cybernetic rule that the efficiency of any controller is related to the quality of its feedback. The efficiency of any rule is based on the quality of its feedback.

Businesses work in much the same way as other CAS because, above a certain size, they ARE CAS. Burger King put SQL servers in every store, but they still couldn’t tell how many hamburgers that they sold each day. A firm can only manage what it can measure – in Holland’s terms, what it can detect through its sensors. It can only act by doing something that it already has the ability to do -- exercising its effectors.

The implication of the Internet revolution is that firms can gather information that they could never gather before, and in a time frame that they could never realistically achieve before. From a web store, a firm can tell which customers buy how often, and how much they spend per order. It is easier to tell which vendors are dependable if you can tell what average delivery times are than if you can’t.

The combination of computing power and networking gives firms the opportunity to grow new sense organs, and to develop new rules for action. The challenge for eManagers is to choose what new things to measure, by divining relationships between outcomes and inputs that weren’t gathered before. If the customers enter their orders into your system, and the vendors enter the invoices, and the financial closings are done in two days instead of two weeks, a firm finds a lot of man-hours available for looking at large databases for those relationships. One of the most likely uses of "data mining" is the assignment of priorities to measures that were never tracked before.

The desired outcome is for managers to have more timely information about where the business actually is, and better indications about where it is going.
